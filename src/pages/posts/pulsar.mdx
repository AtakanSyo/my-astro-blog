  <h2 id="about-this-page">About this page</h2>
  <p>
    Above is a <strong>GPU-accelerated nebula simulation</strong>. Below is a short guide explaining what you’re seeing, how the effect is produced, and how to run it smoothly on your device. 
    The goal is to connect <em>visual intuition</em> with a few practical rendering notes.
  </p>

  <h3 id="what-youre-seeing">What you’re seeing</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Ignition → shell:</strong> A central seed expands into a thin, bright rim.</li>
    <li><strong>Filamentation:</strong> Noise-driven advection and instanced particles evoke tangled, radially stretched strands.</li>
    <li><strong>Additive glow:</strong> Depth writes are disabled so the rim stays smooth without speckle.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2 id="tech-notes">Tech notes (GPU-hybrid)</h2>
  <p>
    On <strong>WebGL2</strong>, tens of thousands of particles render as <em>instanced billboards</em> with additive blending in a single draw call.
    When <strong>WebGPU</strong> is enabled with <code>?gpu=1</code>, particle updates happen in a <em>compute pass</em> and render in one instanced draw, minimizing CPU overhead and improving stability on fast devices.
  </p>

  <h3 id="compatibility">Compatibility</h3>
  <p>
    Works on Chrome, Edge, and Firefox via WebGL2. WebGPU is available on modern Chrome/Edge and recent Safari; otherwise the page stays on WebGL2.
  </p>

  <h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>
  <details>
    <summary>Why does the rim look brightest?</summary>
    <p>High apparent density and additive blending concentrate light at the expanding front, creating a glowing edge.</p>
  </details>
  <details>
    <summary>Is this physically exact?</summary>
    <p>No—this is a stylized visualization focused on motion and texture rather than a full physical simulation.</p>
  </details>
