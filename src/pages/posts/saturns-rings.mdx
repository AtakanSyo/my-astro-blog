  <h3 id="what-youre-seeing">What you’re seeing in the simulation</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Thin, bright rim:</strong> Represents enhanced particle density and forward scattering near ring edges.</li>
    <li><strong>Filamented texture:</strong> Echoes <em>wakes</em> and <em>wavelets</em>—structures driven by gravity, collisions, and resonances.</li>
    <li><strong>Rapid-to-slow expansion:</strong> A stylized nod to differential orbital speeds; inner materials lap the outer rings, stretching features into arcs.</li>
  </ul>

  <aside class="note">

  </aside>

  <h2 id="saturns-rings-quick-tour">Saturn’s rings: a quick tour</h2>
  <p>
    Saturn’s ring system spans hundreds of thousands of kilometers in diameter yet is astonishingly <em>thin</em> (meters to tens of meters in vertical thickness in many regions). 
    The classical labels are <strong>D, C, B, A</strong> (inner to outer), with the dark <strong>Cassini Division</strong> separating the bright B and A rings, and the <strong>F ring</strong> as a narrow, braided band beyond A.
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Composition:</strong> Predominantly water ice with dust and rock mixed in; particle sizes range from powder to boulders.</li>
    <li><strong>Structure drivers:</strong> Self-gravity wakes, resonances with moons (e.g., Mimas), and countless in-ring collisions.</li>
    <li><strong>Color & brightness:</strong> Phase angle and particle size distribution influence the hue and reflectivity you perceive.</li>
  </ul>

  <h3 id="why-the-cassini-division-looks-dark">Why the Cassini Division looks dark</h3>
  <p>
    The Cassini Division isn’t empty; it’s a <em>lower-density</em> region sculpted by resonances. Fewer scatterers along your line of sight → less reflected sunlight → darker appearance at many viewing angles.
  </p>

  <h2 id="observe-saturn-like-a-pro">Observe Saturn like a pro</h2>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Magnification sweet spot:</strong> Often <em>25×–40× per inch</em> of aperture on steady nights. Push higher only when seeing allows.</li>
    <li><strong>Ring tilt matters:</strong> When the rings are more open to Earth, the Cassini Division pops. Near edge-on apparitions, the rings can nearly vanish.</li>
    <li><strong>Filters:</strong> A light yellow or neutral density filter can improve contrast if your sky is bright.</li>
    <li><strong>Timing:</strong> View when Saturn is highest (near transit) to minimize atmospheric blur.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>
  <details>
    <summary>Are Saturn’s rings disappearing?</summary>
    <p>They’re gradually losing mass (micrometeoroid erosion, infall onto Saturn), but on human timescales they’ll remain a showpiece in backyard telescopes.</p>
  </details>
  <details>
    <summary>Why do the rings sometimes look brighter or dimmer?</summary>
    <p>Phase angle, ring tilt, and your local transparency/seeing conditions change the apparent brightness and contrast of ring features.</p>
  </details>
  <details>
    <summary>Can small scopes see the Cassini Division?</summary>
    <p>Yes—under steady skies, ~80–100&nbsp;mm refractors can tease it out at moderate to high magnification when the ring tilt is favorable.</p>
  </details>
