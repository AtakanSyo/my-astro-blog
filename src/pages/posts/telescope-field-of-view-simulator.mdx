<div class="p5-article">
  <h2>How Magnification, TFOV, and Exit Pupil Work</h2>
  <p>
    <strong>Magnification</strong> depends on the telescope and eyepiece focal lengths. Increasing magnification narrows your <strong>true field of view</strong> (TFOV) and decreases the <strong>exit pupil</strong>—the diameter of the light beam exiting the eyepiece.
  </p>
  <p>
    <em>Rules of thumb:</em> Wide AFOV eyepieces show a larger slice of the sky at the same magnification. Exit pupils larger than your eye’s pupil waste light; very small exit pupils dim the image and emphasize floaters or poor seeing.
  </p>

  <h3>Picking Eyepieces</h3>
  <p>
    A practical set might include: a low-power wide-view eyepiece (30–32 mm), a medium-power general eyepiece (15–18 mm), and a high-power planetary eyepiece (6–7 mm). Use the simulator to see how each affects TFOV and exit pupil with your telescope.
  </p>

  <h3>What Fits in the Eyepiece?</h3>
  <p>
    Large objects like the Andromeda Galaxy need wide fields, while compact targets (planets, small nebulae) benefit from higher magnification—if seeing and optics allow.
  </p>

  <h3>Why This Matters</h3>
  <p>
    Choosing the right eyepiece ensures that you maximize both image detail and brightness for the target at hand. With this tool, you can preview your setup before heading out under the stars.
  </p>
