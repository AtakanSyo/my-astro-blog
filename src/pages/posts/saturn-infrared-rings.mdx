<section class="introduction-text">
  <p>
    Saturn’s rings are already beautiful in visible light, but Cassini had a way of
    taking that beauty and pulling back another layer.  
    This image isn’t just a picture — it’s a map in infrared, built with Cassini’s
    Visual and Infrared Mapping Spectrometer (VIMS). It shows us what our eyes can’t,
    and it changes how we think about those iconic rings.
  </p>

<section class="details">
  <h2>What You’re Seeing</h2>
  <p>
    The colors here don’t show what Saturn’s rings really “look like” — they show what
    they’re <em>made of</em>. The bluish and greenish areas are where the water ice is
    purest, with larger grain sizes in the particles. The reddish patches, especially in
    the C ring and the Cassini Division, point to smaller grains or material that’s not
    just ice.
  </p>
  <p>
    On the left, you can see the same data overlaid on a more familiar, natural-color
    image of Saturn. It’s like Cassini is saying: here’s the view you know, and here’s
    the hidden story beneath it.
  </p>
</section>

<section class="context">
  <h2>Why It Matters</h2>
  <p>
    Saturn’s rings aren’t just decoration — they’re an active, changing system. By
    mapping out where the ice is pure and where it’s mixed with other stuff, scientists
    can trace how the rings evolve over time. Things like tiny impacts, contamination
    from micrometeoroids, or even recycling of ring material all leave their signatures
    here.
  </p>
  <p>
    And Saturn isn’t unique in this. These rings are a small-scale example of the same
    physics that shape disks of gas and dust around young stars — the building sites of
    planets. Studying them is like having a laboratory right in our own solar system.
  </p>
</section>
