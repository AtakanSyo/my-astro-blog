<div class="sim-article">
  <h2 id="about-this-page">About this page</h2>
  <p>
    Above is a <strong>GPU-accelerated Neptune visualization</strong>. The scene includes a physically-lit sphere with a 
    subtle Fresnel-based atmospheric rim, realistic axial tilt, and a slow rotation scaled from Neptune’s real day length.
    A dim, inside-out sphere provides the starfield background.
  </p>

  <h3 id="what-youre-seeing">What you’re seeing</h3>
  <p>
    Neptune rotates with a configurable time scale while light from a fixed “Sun” source creates visible phases. 
    The faint blue glow around the edge is an atmospheric effect simulated in a shader, and the stars are part of 
    a large inverted sphere surrounding the planet.
  </p>

  <h2 id="tech-notes">Tech notes</h2>
  <p>
    In <strong>WebGL2</strong> mode, the visualization uses <em>MeshStandardMaterial</em> with ACES tone mapping, sRGB texture loading, 
    and a custom shader hook for slight saturation/contrast adjustments. The atmospheric glow is rendered as a separate 
    back-sided sphere with a Fresnel term, and the starfield uses a basic textured sphere with low brightness.
  </p>

  <h3 id="compatibility">Compatibility</h3>
  <p>
    Works on all modern browsers supporting WebGL2. If WebGPU is enabled and available, the loader will attempt 
    to run that version; otherwise, it falls back to WebGL2 automatically.
  </p>

  <h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>
  <p>
    <strong>Why is the atmosphere faint?</strong> The glow intensity is tuned to be subtle, matching Neptune’s appearance 
    in long-exposure images without overpowering the planet’s details.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Is this physically exact?</strong> No — it’s a stylized real-time visualization designed for smooth performance 
    and visual clarity, rather than a perfect simulation.
  </p>

  <h2 id="credits">Credits</h2>
  <p>
    Based on real-time rendering techniques from modern planetary shaders, inspired by space visualization tools 
    and astrophotography references.
  </p>
</div>
