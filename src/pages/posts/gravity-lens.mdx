<div class="sim-article">
  <h2 id="overview">What this simulation shows</h2>
  <p>
    This interactive model demonstrates <strong>gravitational lensing</strong>: when a massive foreground object
    (e.g., a galaxy) bends light from a more distant <strong>background galaxy</strong>. Depending on alignment and mass,
    the source appears as <strong>multiple images</strong>, <strong>tangential arcs</strong>, or a near-perfect
    <strong>Einstein ring</strong>.
  </p>

  <h3 id="how-it-works">How it works (thin-lens model)</h3>
  <p>
    We use the standard <em>thin-lens approximation</em>, implemented as a full-screen post-processing shader.
    Each pixel corresponds to an angle <code>θ</code> on the image plane. We compute a deflection
    <code>α(θ)</code> caused by the lens, then sample the background source at
    <code>β = θ − α(θ)</code>. For a point-mass lens the magnitude scales like
    <code>|α| ∝ θ_E² / |θ|</code>, where <code>θ_E</code> is the Einstein radius.
    An optional weak <strong>external shear</strong> term imitates the effect of nearby structure, slightly stretching the pattern.
  </p>

  <h3 id="whats-rendered">What’s being rendered</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Source galaxy:</strong> an <em>elliptical Gaussian</em> texture evaluated analytically in the shader (no image files).</li>
    <li><strong>Lens galaxy glow:</strong> a subtle foreground glow in the <em>image plane</em> for context.</li>
    <li><strong>Tone mapping:</strong> ACES to keep highlights pleasant and colors natural across devices.</li>
  </ul>

  <h3 id="controls-performance">Controls & performance</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Controls:</strong> <em>Pause / Resume only</em> (wired to your hard-pause system). No mouse or keyboard input.</li>
    <li><strong>Performance:</strong> single full-screen pass; runs smoothly on modern desktop and mobile GPUs.</li>
    <li><strong>Responsiveness:</strong> resizes with the canvas and preserves aspect so rings stay circular.</li>
  </ul>

  <h3 id="assumptions">Assumptions & limitations</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Thin-lens, single-plane:</strong> no multi-plane lensing or full ray-tracing.</li>
    <li><strong>Point-mass + weak shear:</strong> captures the basic morphology (rings/arcs), not detailed substructure.</li>
    <li><strong>Qualitative scales:</strong> angles and brightness are illustrative; not tied to a specific redshift/cosmology.</li>
  </ul>

  <h3 id="reading-the-image">How to read the image</h3>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Perfect alignment:</strong> source directly behind lens → near-circular <em>Einstein ring</em>.</li>
    <li><strong>Slight offset:</strong> broken ring into bright <em>arcs</em>, typically tangent to a circle of radius ~<code>θ_E</code>.</li>
    <li><strong>Shear present:</strong> arcs appear stretched or slightly squashed along a preferred axis.</li>
  </ul>

  <h2 id="tech-notes">Technical notes</h2>
  <p>
    Built with <strong>Three.js</strong> and <strong>WebGL2</strong>. The scene renders via a single full-screen quad using a
    <em>ShaderMaterial</em> for the deflection and source evaluation. Color output uses sRGB with ACES tone mapping.
    The pause/resume mechanism uses a <code>Clock</code> plus a hard RAF stop to avoid time jumps when resuming.
  </p>
