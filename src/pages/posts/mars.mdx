<div class="sim-article">
  <h2 id="about-this-page">About this page</h2>
  <p>
    Above is a <strong>GPU-accelerated Mars visualization</strong>. The scene includes a physically-lit sphere textured with realistic reddish-brown terrain colors, darker volcanic plains, 
    and brighter dust-covered regions. A faint polar ice cap effect is visible near the poles. A dim, inside-out sphere provides the starfield background.
  </p>

  <h3 id="what-youre-seeing">What you’re seeing</h3>
  <p>
    Mars rotates at a speed close to Earth’s day length, so its surface features drift past at a comfortable pace. The planet’s axial tilt produces gentle seasonal lighting effects,
    and the simulated Sun creates visible shading across the surface.
  </p>

  <h2 id="tech-notes">Tech notes</h2>
  <p>
    In <strong>WebGL2</strong> mode, the visualization uses <em>MeshStandardMaterial</em> with ACES tone mapping, sRGB texture loading, and a small shader hook for color grading.
    The polar caps are blended procedurally, and the starfield is a simple low-brightness textured sphere.
    If WebGPU is enabled, the loader will attempt that path; otherwise, it falls back to WebGL2 automatically.
  </p>

  <h3 id="compatibility">Compatibility</h3>
  <p>
    Works on all modern browsers supporting WebGL2. If WebGPU is enabled and available, the loader will attempt 
    to run that version; otherwise, it falls back to WebGL2 automatically.
  </p>

  <h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>
  <p>
    <strong>Are the surface details real?</strong> The colors and broad features are based on Mars spacecraft imagery, but this is a stylized real-time rendering, not a photogrammetric model.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Is the polar cap accurate?</strong> It’s an artistic approximation, not a physically evolving seasonal cap.
  </p>

  <h2 id="credits">Credits</h2>

    Inspired by Mars imagery from NASA, ESA, and planetary visualization tools, adapted for smooth WebGL2 rendering.
  </p>
